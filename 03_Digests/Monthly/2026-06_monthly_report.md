# Monthly Report — June 2026

**Scope:** Conferences, software projects, hardware vendors, benchmarks, and datasets monitored per `00_Config/sources.yaml`. This is the Observatory's first monthly synthesis cycle; the knowledge base is still thin (one weekly digest, 11 seed records in `01_Knowledge_Base/`), so this report leans more heavily on ecosystem-level signals than on cross-paper synthesis. Future monthly reports should have a deeper backlog of weekly digests to draw trend lines from.

## Emerging trends

The clearest signal this month is not a single paper or chip but a quiet architectural argument happening across the toolchain layer: the industry is bifurcating between general-purpose ML compilers retreating from microcontroller-class targets, and hardware vendors pushing AI processing further down into the sensor itself. On the compiler side, Apache TVM's v0.25.0 release candidate (uploaded 9 June 2026) includes a runtime refactor explicitly described as removing "leftover microTVM/CRT crumbs" — consistent with an open community discussion thread on the TVM forum questioning microTVM's future development. Read together, these are not proof that microTVM is being discontinued, but they are a real and verifiable signal that the project's bare-metal/CRT-specific code paths are being pared down rather than invested in. That matters directly for our [[microTVM]] and [[TVM]] taxonomy entries: if the trend continues, frameworks like CMSIS-NN and TensorFlow Lite Micro — which target the MCU tier directly rather than through a general compiler — may consolidate their position as the practical default for ultra-low-power deployment, while TVM's center of gravity drifts toward GPU/accelerator-class hardware.

At the opposite end of the stack, hardware vendors are pushing intelligence into the sensor, not just the MCU. STMicroelectronics' IIS3DWB10IS vibration sensor (announced 3 June 2026) embeds a second-generation "Intelligent Sensor Processing Unit" (ISPU 2.0, 40 MIPS/40 MFLOPS) directly on the MEMS die, explicitly positioned as a piezosensor alternative for industrial condition monitoring. This is a concrete data point for an "in-sensor AI" trend that sits below even our current Hardware taxonomy (Cortex-M, Cortex-A, RISC-V, DSP, FPGA, NPU) — a category boundary worth revisiting, since "computation inside the sensor package" is a distinct deployment target from "computation on an MCU fed by a sensor."

A third, broader trend visible in the wider edge AI discourse this month — without yet being represented in any of our taxonomy branches — is the push to run small language and vision-language models directly on edge silicon, evidenced by NVIDIA's published Jetson Thor token-throughput benchmarks for Mistral and Qwen-family models. This is a different weight class from the Cortex-M-class deployments this Observatory mostly tracks, but it is converging on similar concerns (quantization, memory footprint, latency) and is worth a dedicated taxonomy discussion rather than being silently absorbed into existing categories.

## New research directions

Building on the three papers already surfaced in the 29 June weekly digest, two directions look ready for deeper investment. First, on-device-runnable NAS search procedures (as in the ultra-low-power HW-NAS paper, arXiv:2606.16290) point toward a broader question: which parts of the model-development pipeline can be moved onto the constrained device itself, not just inference? Second, the disentangled ablation methodology used in the NVFP4 quantization paper (arXiv:2606.06527) — separating the contribution of scaling from the contribution of bit width — is a template that could productively be applied to other compression techniques in our [[Quantization]] and [[Compression]] branches, many of which report end-to-end accuracy numbers without isolating which design choice actually drove the result.

## Influential research groups

The evidence base is too thin this month to make strong claims about which groups are leading specific subfields — this is a function of the Observatory having only one weekly digest of papers reviewed so far, not of the field lacking leaders. Worth flagging for future months: the NVFP4 quantization paper's author list (Sen, Kamineni, Lobo, Bhunia, Ewetz, Chatterjee) spans groups with prior work in hardware-software co-design, and the HW-NAS paper (Garavagno, Ragusa, Frisoli, Gastaldo) comes from a group with a track record in embedded/wearable robotics rather than pure ML — both worth tracking across future submissions rather than treating as one-off.

## Research and thesis opportunities

Three concrete directions emerge from this month's synthesis, each tied to existing taxonomy branches. First, a systematic comparison of MCU-native inference libraries (CMSIS-NN, TFLite Micro) against general compiler-based toolchains (TVM/microTVM) as the latter's bare-metal investment appears to soften — a thesis could measure whether this is already showing up as a performance or maintenance gap on real Cortex-M targets, directly extending [[Frameworks]] and [[Cortex-M]]. Second, in-sensor AI processing (as in STMicroelectronics' ISPU 2.0) opens a question about where the optimal compute boundary sits for specific applications — a Master's-level study comparing energy and latency trade-offs of in-sensor versus on-MCU inference for a fixed task (e.g., vibration-based predictive maintenance, connecting to our [[Predictive Maintenance]] application branch) would produce genuinely new comparative data rather than restating vendor claims. Third, the disentangled-ablation methodology from the NVFP4 paper could be formalized into a reusable evaluation protocol for compression technique papers generally — a methodological contribution rather than a new algorithm, but one with real value for reproducibility in the [[Quantization]] and [[Compression]] literature, and a reasonable scope for an undergraduate or early Master's thesis.
